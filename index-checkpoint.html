1979 — Baseline

The Era of Ice

Satellite monitoring begins. The Arctic Ocean is locked in thick, multi-year ice connecting Russia, Alaska, Canada, and Greenland in one continuous frozen expanse. The September minimum covers roughly 7.1 million km².

7.1M km² extent
– First satellite record
2005 — Accelerating

The Shrink Begins

The extent drops to 5.5 million km². Wide expanses of open ocean begin appearing off the coasts of Siberia and Alaska through the late-summer months, opening shipping routes that were once permanently frozen.

5.5M km² extent
−23% Loss from 1979
2012 — Record Minimum

The Catastrophic Melt

A devastating season. Ice plummets to just 3.39 million km² — the lowest ever recorded in human history. The Northwest Passage opens to unescorted shipping for the first time. Arctic amplification is now undeniable.

3.4M km² extent
−52% All-time record loss
2024 — The New Normal

A Permanently Changed World

While slightly above the 2012 record, 4.2 million km² is now the expected baseline. The ice that remains is dangerously thin and seasonal. Every single year since 2007 ranks among the 20 lowest extents ever measured.

4.2M km² extent
−41% vs. 1979 baseline

The Ice Doesn't Lie

Four decades of satellite data tell a single, unambiguous story. The Arctic is warming four times faster than the global average — and the sea ice is its most visible casualty.
